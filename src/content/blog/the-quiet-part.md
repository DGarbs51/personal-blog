A few months ago, I published a blog post about choosing to stay. About loyalty. About being a finisher. About tending the fire through the slow seasons. I meant every word of it.

Then I left.

I don't think that makes me a liar. I think it makes me someone who wrote down what he believed at the time — and then watched the ground shift underneath it.

## The Cracks

After I wrote that post, things didn't get better. Some of them got worse. I'm not going to drag anyone through the details because the people I worked with deserved better than that. But I'll tell you one story that says everything.

I was building with AI agents. The results were real — we're talking a feature estimated to save the firm millions of dollars a year. To keep building, I needed access to a premium model. Twenty-five bucks a month. That's it. And the process to get that approved was going to take _months_.

That's not a complaint about bureaucracy. Every big company has process. But when you're staring at something that obvious — a $25 tool, millions in value, and a timeline measured in quarters just to get a credit card charge approved — you start to wonder if the system you're inside is even capable of wanting what you're trying to give it.

## The Onsie

My son Myles was born in late July 2025. Seven weeks later, I started at Laravel.

In between those two things, a decision happened. And the way it happened is the part I keep coming back to.

Laravel didn't just offer me a job. They sent my newborn a onsie. They gave me time to think. They didn't pressure me or pitch me — they just said, _we want you_, and then they waited. They treated me like a human being making a life decision, not a requisition to close.

I've been in rooms where hiring is a transaction. This wasn't that. And I didn't realize how much that mattered to me until someone showed me what it looked like.

## The Hardest Part

Choosing was hard. I won't pretend it wasn't. You don't spend years building something, leading people, lighting a fire and tending it — and then walk away clean. That's not how it works.

But the hardest part wasn't choosing. The hardest part was not being able to say goodbye.

I'll leave it there.

## The Thing That Made It Different

I wouldn't have left for anyone but Laravel. I need you to understand that. This wasn't a "grass is greener" move. It wasn't burnout. It wasn't chasing a title or a paycheck.

It was Laravel. The framework that lit my career on fire six years earlier. The ecosystem that taught me what building could feel like when the tools matched the way I think. And now they wanted me — not as a developer, but as a Solutions Engineer. Someone who could sit across from another builder and help them see what I already knew.

If any other company had called, I'd still be where I was. I'm sure of it.

## The Messy Part

Here's where most people would write the montage. New job, fresh start, everything clicking into place. That's not what happened.

My first thirty days at Laravel, I worked support tickets. Not because something went wrong — it was the right call. Learn the product, learn the customers, learn the sharp edges. But it's a strange feeling to go from leading an engineering org to answering tickets. Your ego has opinions about that, even when your brain knows better.

Two weeks in, new leadership came in and the whole sales motion shifted. The previous leaders had hired seasoned Laravel developers who could learn to sell. The new leaders wanted seasoned sellers. Suddenly I was in a role that was being redefined around me before I'd even figured out the first version.

I was brand new to sales. Brand new to startups. Brand new to a company that was itself figuring out what it wanted to be. Nothing was clear. Everything was moving.

And honestly? That's the part I'd been missing.

## The Quiet Part

I told myself I was a finisher. And maybe I am — for the things that still deserve to be finished. But I think what I actually learned is that loyalty isn't the same as staying. Sometimes loyalty means being honest about when your chapter is done, even if the book isn't.

The decision was right for me and for my family. Whether it was the best thing for the team and friends I left behind — I genuinely don't know. Maybe it was. Maybe me leaving opened a door for someone who needed it. Maybe it didn't. I don't get to write that part of the story.

What I do know is that I'm building again. Not the same way — not writing every line of code, not owning the architecture, not leading a team of engineers. I'm sitting across from people who build software and helping them see what's possible. It's a different kind of building. But it's still building.

And I'm still not done yet.
